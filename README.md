# What Clean Actually Means
### Canadian Cosmetics Regulatory Compliance Analysis

I picked up a skincare product at a pharmacy and the front said clean beauty and clinically proven to reduce fine lines. I started wondering what those phrases actually mean in Canada and whether anyone checks them before a product goes on shelf.

The answer is that there are real rules. And a lot of products are not meeting them.

---

## What this is

A regulatory affairs and compliance analysis of how fictional Canadian beauty products measure up against Health Canada's Cosmetics Regulations. 8 regulatory requirements mapped. 15 products audited with a compliance score for each. 10 marketing claims assessed against what Health Canada actually says. A sample of Cosmetic Ingredient Hotlist entries. A pre-launch compliance checklist for any company wanting to bring a cosmetic product to market in Canada.

## What the analysis found

The average compliance score across 15 audited products is 56.4 out of 100. Eight of the 15 have at least one significant compliance gap. The most common and most serious is also the least known, making claims that legally classify a product as a drug while selling it as a cosmetic.

10 of the 15 products contain claims that trigger drug classification under the Food and Drugs Act. SPF claims. Hair growth claims. Healing and repair claims. Products making these claims require a Drug Identification Number from Health Canada before they can be sold. They do not have one.

4 products have no Health Canada cosmetic notification on file, required within 10 days of first sale. 3 products have English-only labels. Bilingual labelling is mandatory in Canada.

The claims analysis shows that terms like clean, natural, non-toxic, and hypoallergenic have no legal definition in Canadian cosmetics law. Any brand can use them without meeting any standard. Two terms; SPF and heals or repairs are not unregulated marketing language. They are drug claims.

## What the five tabs cover

The Regulatory Landscape maps all eight requirements with the regulatory reference, most common gap, and risk of non-compliance for each.

The Product Compliance Audit shows all 15 products with their compliance scores, a breakdown of gap types, and a detailed view of each product's specific gaps.

The Claims Problem shows the ten marketing terms assessed with risk levels, how many products use each term, and what Health Canada actually says about it.

The Ingredient Hotlist shows a sample of ten Hotlist entries with prohibited and restricted classifications and consumer risk information.

The Pre-Launch Checklist is a practical ten-step guide for a regulatory affairs analyst preparing a new cosmetic product for the Canadian market.
